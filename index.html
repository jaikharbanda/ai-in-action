The take-home version

Missed the live game, or want to test a colleague? In each pair below, one message was written by a person, one by AI. Pick, then reveal.

A. “Bit of a weird one, but bear with me. Last Tuesday I locked myself out of the office with a client due in ten minutes. What happened next changed how I think about our front-desk process.”
B. “In today’s fast-paced digital landscape, adaptability isn’t just a buzzword — it’s a survival skill. Here are 5 lessons every professional should embrace.”
B is the AI. The tells: stock phrases, uniform enthusiasm, zero specifics. The human one has a time, a place and a problem.
A. “I sincerely apologise for any inconvenience this may have caused. Please rest assured that we are committed to ensuring this does not happen again.”
B. “Sorry, completely my fault, I misread the invite. Can we grab 15 minutes tomorrow instead? I’ll bring the numbers you asked for.”
A is the AI. Over-formal, apologises to nobody in particular, promises nothing concrete. The human names the mistake and fixes it.
A. “We fixed the bug where exports timed out on large files. If it still happens, reply here and we’ll dig in.”
B. “We’re thrilled to announce a suite of powerful new enhancements designed to supercharge your workflow and unlock seamless productivity!”
B is the AI. “Thrilled”, “supercharge”, “seamless”, and not one word about what changed.

The point isn’t that AI writing is bad. Unedited, generic AI writing is easy to spot. Tonight is about using it well instead.

Tonight’s prompts

1. The one-sentence app build (Lovable)

Build a simple expense-splitting app for a group dinner. People add who paid for what, and the app works out who owes whom, with a one-tap settle-up summary. Mobile-first, warm colours, playful but professional. No login.

Swap the first sentence for your own idea. The same habit works in Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini or Copilot: describe the outcome, not the implementation, then iterate out loud.

2. Teach AI to sound like you

Below are three real examples of how I write (an email, a chat message, and a longer doc). Study them and describe my voice back to me: tone, sentence length, sign-offs, favourite phrases, and words I would never use. Ask me three questions about anything you can't tell from the samples. Then, from now on, draft everything in my voice.

3. The hand-off rule

Hand off to AI

Repetitive and rule-based · easy to check at a glance · cheap to get wrong once · first drafts, summaries, legwork

Keep human

Judgment calls and trade-offs · relationships and trust · anything you’d have to apologise for · the final quality bar

The intern test: would you give the task to a bright intern on day one? Then let AI take the first pass, and check it like an intern’s work.
